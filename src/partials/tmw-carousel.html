• 1

    Look vs. individual items

    Choosing between buying a whole look or an individual item shapes the flow significantly. (Following is look-flow)

  • 2

    Purpose builds trust

    Acknowledging the shopper’s purpose creates trust that the retailer can solve their problem

  • 3

    Formality shapes recommendation type

    Formality means the difference between recommending a suit vs. a sweater, and allows asking about attributes of specific items.

  • 4

    Colors represent style

    Style is abstract, but color (and pattern) helps users concretely communicate their style preference.

  • 5

    Fit by body type

    Without the ability to measure shoppers, a self-assessment of body type from “classic” to “extreme slim” narrows sizes.

  • 6

    Size specifies fit

    An optional step is that shoppers can enter sizes they already know for all relevant products, ensuring all recommendations are accurate.

  • 7

    Collections reflect budget

    To keep shoppers from only choosing low prices and refocus them on what they are actually looking for, collection are used to connect price to quality.

  • 8

    Jackets shapes pairing direction

    The first recommendation is the jacket - the main piece that defines the look of the rest of the outfit.

  • 9

    pairing happens visually

    Multiple “core” categories are shown alongside the jacket, invoking a mirror-like visual, and reducing question-fatigue.

  • 10

    deals close sales

    Once shoppers choose the items they want, they are emotionally invested in the products and are looking to get the best deal.

  • 11

    previews fine-tune details

    Shoppers feeling hesitant to check-out without reviewing every detail can preview their product and make adjustments.
